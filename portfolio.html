GEOG 490 Portfolio: A Spatial Story of the Seattle Region

This portfolio brings together my work from GEOG 490 into one larger spatial story about population, inequality, segregation, and spatial modeling in the Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue metropolitan region. Instead of treating each lab as a separate assignment, I organized the portfolio so that each section builds on the last. The early labs introduce census data and basic visualization, while the later labs use spatial statistics and modeling to explain deeper geographic patterns.

The main story of this portfolio is that population patterns are not random. Where people live, where income is concentrated, where racial and ethnic groups are more or less represented, and where model results change across space all reveal important information about the social geography of the Seattle region. The maps and graphs in this portfolio show how data science and GIS can work together to make those patterns visible.

Lab 2 – Exploratory Data Analysis

NHGIS Total Population by State

Lab 2 begins with exploratory data analysis using census population data. This graph compares total population by state using NHGIS data. It helped me practice downloading census data, checking column names, cleaning the dataset, and turning raw data into a readable visualization.

This graph is important because it shows how large-scale population data can provide context before narrowing in on one specific region. By starting at the state level, I was able to understand how population varies across the United States before focusing more closely on the Seattle metropolitan area in later labs.

tidycensus Total Population by State

This chart uses tidycensus to pull 2020 Decennial Census population data directly into R. Compared with the NHGIS workflow, tidycensus made it easier to access census data through code instead of manually downloading files.

The purpose of this graph was not only to show population by state, but also to compare two different data workflows. This became useful later in the portfolio because many of the maps depend on using tidycensus to gather demographic variables quickly and accurately.

Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue Metro Population

This chart introduces the Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue metropolitan area as the main geography of interest for the portfolio. Choosing one metro area gave the rest of the project a consistent location and helped connect each lab into a single story.

Seattle is a useful case study because it contains dense urban neighborhoods, suburban communities, and less populated areas. This makes it a strong region for exploring population concentration, income inequality, segregation, and spatial modeling.

Lab 3 – Census Mapping and Visualization

Total Population Choropleth

This choropleth map shows total population across King County, Washington. Darker census tracts represent areas with more residents, while lighter tracts show lower population totals. The map shows that population is not evenly distributed across the county.

The strongest population concentration appears around Seattle and nearby urban and suburban communities. In contrast, the eastern portion of King County appears much less populated. This pattern reflects the difference between the dense urban corridor near Seattle and the more mountainous, less developed areas farther east.

Dot Density Map

The dot density map tells the population story in a different way. Instead of shading entire census tracts, it uses dots to represent people. This makes it easier to visually understand where population is clustered.

Dense groups of dots appear around Seattle and surrounding communities, showing where people are most concentrated. The map also helps show that population density changes gradually across the region rather than stopping neatly at census tract boundaries. This visualization is useful because it feels more immediate and visual than a standard choropleth map.

Young Persons Graduated Symbols

This graduated symbol map focuses on young people under age 18. Larger circles represent tracts with higher numbers of young residents. This adds another layer to the portfolio by showing that population patterns are not only about where people live, but also about who lives there.

Areas with larger symbols may reflect places with more families, schools, and suburban residential neighborhoods. Comparing this map with the total population map helps show whether young people are concentrated in the same places as the general population or whether they follow a different spatial pattern.

Lab 5 – Spatial Analysis and Modeling

Metropolitan Census Tracts

This map introduces the idea of metropolitan geography. Instead of only looking at one city or county, metropolitan regions include many connected communities that function as part of the same urban system.

Mapping census tracts within the metro boundary helped me understand how geographic boundaries shape spatial analysis. A metro area can include dense urban cores, suburban neighborhoods, and outer areas with different population and income patterns. This matters because the results of spatial analysis can change depending on the boundary used.

Income Hotspot Analysis

This hotspot analysis identifies statistically significant clusters of high and low median household income. Unlike a regular income map, this analysis does more than show where values are high or low. It shows where similar income values are spatially clustered.

The map helps reveal regional inequality by showing that income patterns are geographically organized. High-income clusters suggest areas where wealth is concentrated, while low-income clusters point to areas where economic disadvantage may be more spatially concentrated. This lab helped me move from simple mapping toward spatial statistics.

Lab 7 – Segregation and Diversity

Seattle MSA Segregation Map

This map examines segregation across the Seattle metropolitan area using demographic data for White, Black, Asian, and Hispanic populations. It adds a social interpretation to the portfolio by showing that racial and ethnic groups are not distributed evenly across space.

The map helps show how diversity and segregation can exist at the same time. A metro area may be diverse overall, but individual neighborhoods can still show uneven patterns of concentration and separation. This visualization connects census data to larger questions about housing, access, neighborhood change, and social inequality.

Asian Location Quotient

This location quotient map shows where Asian residents are more concentrated compared with the regional average. A higher location quotient means that the Asian population makes up a larger share of that tract than it does across the metro area overall.

This map is useful because it focuses on relative concentration rather than raw population totals. A tract does not have to contain the largest number of people to have a high location quotient. Instead, the map shows where Asian residents have a stronger relative presence, helping reveal community patterns that may be hidden in a basic population map.

Alternative Asian LQ Visualization

This alternative version provides another way of visualizing Asian population concentration across the region. Including a second version helped me think about how design choices affect interpretation.

Even when the underlying data stays the same, changes in color, classification, and layout can make certain patterns easier or harder to see. This reminded me that cartography is not just technical. It also involves communication choices that shape how viewers understand the data.

Lab 9 – Spatial Modeling

Geographically Weighted Regression Income Map

The geographically weighted regression map shows that relationships between variables can change across space. Instead of assuming that one model explains the entire Seattle metropolitan area, GWR allows the relationship to vary from place to place.

This is important because urban regions are not uniform. A factor that helps explain income patterns in one part of the metro area may not work the same way somewhere else. This map adds depth to the portfolio because it shows how spatial modeling can reveal local variation that a single global model might miss.

K-Means Cluster Map

This k-means cluster map groups census tracts based on similar characteristics. Instead of looking at one variable at a time, clustering helps identify areas that share similar demographic or socioeconomic profiles.

The clusters create a final layer in the portfolio story by showing how spatial patterns can be classified and summarized. The map helps identify groups of tracts that may represent different types of neighborhoods or regional zones. This lab shows how GIS and data science can work together to move from description toward pattern recognition.
