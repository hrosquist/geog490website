GEOG 490 Portfolio

Demographic and Economic Patterns in the Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue Metropolitan Area

Introduction

For my GEOG 490 portfolio, I chose to focus on the Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue metropolitan area in Washington. This region is useful for demographic analysis because it includes a large urban core, surrounding suburbs, and meaningful variation in population, race and ethnicity, income, and spatial inequality. Using census data and spatial analysis methods in R, this portfolio tells a story about how demographic and economic patterns are distributed across the metropolitan area.

Geography of Interest

The Seattle metropolitan area is an appropriate case study because it contains dense urban neighborhoods, suburban communities, and areas with different levels of income and demographic diversity. Census tracts are used as the main unit of analysis because they are small enough to show neighborhood-level variation while still being commonly used for ACS demographic and economic data.

Figure 1: Median Household Income

This map shows median household income across census tracts in the Seattle area. The map helps show that economic patterns are not evenly distributed across the region. Some areas have higher income levels, while other tracts show lower median household income. This figure adds to the case study by showing the economic geography of the metropolitan area and helping connect demographic patterns to broader questions of inequality and access.

Figure 2: Total Population

This map displays total population by census tract. It shows where population is most concentrated within the Seattle metropolitan area. Population patterns are important because they help explain where demographic changes are most visible and where infrastructure, housing, transportation, and services may be most needed.

Figure 3: Race and Ethnicity Dot Density Map

This dot density map shows the distribution of selected racial and ethnic groups across the Seattle area. Instead of showing only tract-level percentages, the dot density method helps visualize where people are located and how groups are distributed across space. This map supports the portfolio narrative by showing that race and ethnicity are spatially patterned within the metropolitan area.

Figure 4: Young Persons Graduated Symbol Map

This map uses graduated symbols to show the distribution of young people across the Seattle area. Larger symbols indicate places with higher numbers of young residents. This figure helps show how age-related demographic patterns vary across the region and adds another layer to understanding population composition.

Figure 5: Segregation and Diversity

This map examines segregation and diversity across the Seattle metropolitan area. Segregation analysis helps show whether different racial and ethnic groups are evenly distributed or concentrated in particular areas. This method is important because it moves beyond basic population counts and helps identify broader patterns of separation, inequality, and uneven access within the region.

Figure 6: Asian Location Quotient

This location quotient map focuses on the Asian population in the Seattle metropolitan area. A location quotient shows where a group is more or less concentrated compared with the region overall. This map helps identify places where the Asian population has a stronger relative presence and supports a more detailed interpretation of racial and ethnic geography.

Figure 7: Geographically Weighted Regression

The geographically weighted regression analysis shows that relationships between demographic and economic variables can vary across space. Instead of assuming one relationship applies equally across the entire metropolitan area, GWR helps show how local patterns may differ from one part of the region to another. This adds methodological depth to the portfolio because it connects spatial modeling to the demographic story.

Figure 8: K-Means Clustering

The k-means cluster map groups census tracts with similar demographic and economic characteristics. This method helps summarize complex data into clearer spatial categories. The clusters make it easier to see how different types of neighborhoods appear across the Seattle metropolitan area and how population, race, income, and other variables combine into broader patterns.

Conclusion

Overall, the Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue metropolitan area shows clear spatial variation in population, race and ethnicity, income, segregation, and demographic clustering. The maps and methods in this portfolio show that demographic change is not evenly distributed across space. By combining census data, mapping, and spatial analysis, this project demonstrates how geographic methods can be used to tell a clearer story about urban inequality and metropolitan change.
