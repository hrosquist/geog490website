GEOG 490 Portfolio: A Spatial Story of the Seattle Region

This portfolio follows my work through GEOG 490 by building a spatial story about population, inequality, segregation, and modeling in the Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue metropolitan area. Each lab adds another layer: first mapping census patterns, then analyzing spatial relationships, then interpreting segregation and diversity, and finally using spatial modeling to identify deeper geographic patterns.

Lab 3 – Census Mapping and Visualization

Total Population Choropleth

This choropleth map begins the story by showing where people live across King County, Washington. Darker census tracts show higher total population, while lighter areas show fewer residents. The pattern reveals a dense western urban corridor near Seattle and more sparsely populated areas toward the eastern part of the county.

Dot Density Map

The dot density map adds detail by showing population distribution as individual dots. Instead of only comparing tract totals, this map makes the concentration of people easier to see visually. Dense clusters appear around Seattle and nearby suburbs, while the eastern portion of the county has fewer dots and lower population density.

Young Persons Graduated Symbols

This graduated symbol map focuses on the population under age 18. Larger circles show census tracts with more young residents. This helps show how family-oriented communities and suburban areas may have different demographic patterns than central urban neighborhoods.

Lab 5 – Spatial Analysis and Modeling

Portland-Vancouver Metro Tracts

This map introduces metropolitan geography by showing census tracts across the Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro area. The metro boundary shows how urban regions can extend across state lines and include many connected communities. This lab helped me think about how boundaries shape spatial analysis.

Income Hotspot Analysis

This hotspot analysis identifies statistically significant clusters of high and low median household income in the Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue metropolitan area. The map moves beyond simple description by showing where income patterns cluster spatially, making regional inequality more visible.

Lab 7 – Segregation and Diversity

Seattle MSA Segregation Map

This map examines segregation across the Seattle metropolitan area using demographic data for White, Black, Asian, and Hispanic populations. It adds a social interpretation to the portfolio by showing that population groups are not distributed evenly across space.

Asian Location Quotient

This location quotient map measures where Asian residents are more concentrated compared with the regional average. Higher values show neighborhoods where the Asian population has a stronger relative presence. This map helps connect demographic concentration to broader questions about settlement, community, and urban change.

Alternative Asian LQ Visualization

This alternative version provides another way of visualizing Asian population concentration across the region.

Lab 9 – Spatial Modeling

GWR Income Map

The geographically weighted regression map shows how relationships between variables can change across space. Instead of assuming one relationship applies to the entire region, GWR helps reveal local variation within the Seattle metropolitan area.

K-Means Cluster Map

This k-means cluster map groups census tracts based on similar demographic and spatial characteristics. The clusters create a final layer in the portfolio story by showing how statistical modeling can identify geographic patterns that may not be obvious from one map alone.
